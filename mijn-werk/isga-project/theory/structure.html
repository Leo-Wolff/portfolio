Sentence Structure

In the Dutch language the structure of sentences changes depending on the context. The structure can change in three different forms:

  • The base
  • Secondary sentence
  • Inversion

The base

The base sentence structure of the Dutch language is the same as most European languages:
Subject Verb Object

Note: For ease of explanation we will be calling object something differently because we are actually referring to the rest of the sentence. It will be called rest

Example:
Jan loopt weg.
Jan walks away.

Secondary sentence

Think about words such as: but, because, while, etc. In Dutch, this causes the sentence structure to change. Do note that the sentence structure before the secondary sentence stays the same.

The sentence structure of a secondary sentence is as follows:
Subject Rest Verb

Example:
Daarom liep Jan weg.
Therefore Jan walked away.

Example (in a full sentence):
Het regende buiten, daarom liep Jan weg.
It rained outside, therefore Jan walked away.

Inversion

If there is any word in front of the basic sentence structure, the structure gets inverted. This can happen with any word as long as it doesn’t fit the criteria for the secondary sentence structure. Popular examples are time placement, words such as: tomorrow, yesterday, last week, etc.

The structure gets inverted like so:
Verb Subject Rest

Example:
Morgen loopt Jan weg.
Tomorrow, Jan walks away.
